Introduction

“Take me to church, I'll worship like a dog at the shrine of your lies, I'll tell you my sins and you can sharpen your knife, offer me that deathless death, good God, let me give you my life” (Hozier, 2014). I imagine that this phrase may have rang a bell in your head if you are familiar with recent contemporary music. If that is not the case, I ask that you take a seat and get your YouTube search box ready for action. The words quoted above are the lyrics of a song titled “Take Me to Church”, in which well-known Irish musician Hozier, created a music video taking the power of music and visual rhetoric to an entirely new level of protest on love, religion, and hate.

Hozier’s “Take me to Church” music video redefines the iconicity of religious love by juxtaposing images of crime and sexuality while challenging the othering of homosexual individuals in religious worship. Together, visual strategies and context within the lyrics of the song create active meaning through anchorage techniques. All of these strategies combine to create an image of activism, challenging religions ability to undermine sexuality and shame uncontrollable human tendencies.  

In this essay I will visually analyze Hozier’s music video using concepts of visual rhetoric and further discuss the producer’s techniques which mobilize communication. This essay will be broken down by three main parts, context, analysis, and implications. In the historical context of the image, I will define how meaning is articulated through context and visual aesthetics. Through an analysis of visual strategies, I will argue central concepts that are functioning as the major rhetorical force in the image and close by discussing implications in connection to the music video.

Context

In order to further analyze this music video, it is important that an understanding of visual rhetoric is made so that I can relay what I believe to be the meaning behind this image, being the music video. With that being said, it is important to emphasize that although I am analyzing a music video, it is still considered a visual image. According to rhetorical scholars Helmer’s and Hill, visual rhetoric can be defined by “The human use of signs to communicate and the mobilization of these signs for the creation of identities, ideologies, consciousness, communities, publics, and cultures” (Helmer & Hill, 2004). In other words, visual rhetoric is often not something that can be stated directly within the image or video. Signs, both visible and not function as communicators which mobilize a specific thought or idea in communication. This music video is made up completely of signs which are not completely visible to the eye.   

In Hozier’s music video, some of the signs are very important to the intended meaning. even in the simplest form. Believe it or not, an entire science has been dedicated to these signs known to rhetorical scholars as semiology or semiotics. According to one of the leading theorists in semiology, Roland Barthes states, “Semiology aims to take in any system of signs, whatever their substance and limits; images, gestures, musical sounds, objects, and the complex associations of all these, which form the content of ritual, convention or public entertainment” (Barthes, 1964). These signs can be further explained with direct connection to the content of the music video.

Published to YouTube on September 25, 2013, Hozier’s, “Take Me to Church” was self-written following a breakup to his longtime girlfriend. The single reached the top of the Irish charts a few weeks after its release (Tingen, 2015). In short, the music video pictures a gay couple who are forced to hide their love for one another because of strict religious practices. Living in fear of being caught, eventually their secret is found by people of their religion and they are brutally punished by flame and torture. In the end it is implied that they were killed or bound to burn in hell for the sin that they have committed. In an interview with The Cut, Hozier stated that “the song is about asserting yourself and reclaiming your humanity through an act of love" (Song Facts, 2016).

The video relied heavily on the articulation of signs. In order to understand the true meaning behind this music video you must know the difference between the signifier and the signified. As quoted by Rolland Barthes, “The signified and the signifier, in Saussurean terminology, are the components of the sign (Barthes, 1964). According to a text from Changing Minds.org, the authors write, “It is in the interpretation of the signifier that meaning is created. The signified is the meaning, the thing indicated by the signifier (Minds.org, 2016).

In Hozier’s music video, the fire at the end in which we see the two men being taken to works as the signifier to imply that the boys are being punished. The signified is much deeper which is when it is signified that because of their sexuality, they will burn in hell. As I stated before, the video works as a metaphor to be interpreted through sign. Being that I am analyzing the meaning behind the video, I think it is also important to know and implement the ways of shifting meaning. According to class lecture, we learned that denotation is the literal meaning of an image and connotation is the cultural meaning (Light, 2016). Watching the video, you can easily understand the denotation that is two homosexual men being subject to a hate crime. The connotative meaning again goes much further in that it focuses more on the religious cultures associated with sexual orientation and takes into account the significance and relevance in contemporary views.

Analysis

The four major rhetorical forces of this music video consist of iconicity, juxtaposition, othering, and anchorage and relay. With a resistance to dominant ideologies, the music video challenges and redefines what we are taught about sexuality and religion. In doing so, Hozier’s video juxtaposes images of homosexual relationship to religious practices and expectations using hate crime as sign to signify the religion. This video also challenges the othering of Homosexual individuals in religious worship by blatantly using graphic images to let the issue be known. One of the major concepts that seems to be driving the rhetorical force of the video significantly is anchorage. As we know from lecture, anchorage is text that fixes the meaning of an image (Light, 2016). In Hozier’s video, the meaning of the words is being anchored by the visuals.

In this depiction of sexuality and religious tensions, we begin to see a major shift in terms of the iconicity of religious love. The producer of the video chose to leave out what society deems to be a “normal” relationship. Instead viewers are graced with the redefinition of love and religion. That redefinition depicts two homosexual males kissing and hanging out with one another as days pass until they are caught by their religion and taken to the fire after being beat. This also challenges heteronormativity which promotes the idea that heterosexual relationships are of the only norm in our society.

What visual strategies work to redefine the iconicity of religious love in Hozier’s video? In the video alone, we began to see this relationship of religion and love be redefined. Through juxtaposing images where groups of men are on the hunt to hurt the gay men being shown and intimate kissing between the two lovers is being shown can we articulate a contrasting effect that challenges traditional views of what love should be in religion. To be more specific, the gay men choose to hide their love which gives us a glance into what it may be like to have to hide such a big aspect of who you are. This alone can also word to redefine what we deem as normal in terms of religion and love. One might say that a person can lead by example, so we can only hope that these images can make a lasting impact on homophobia or harmful religious tendencies.

As I stated before, the video uses images to symbolize certain parts of the signified storyline. In the video we see a group of men which seem to be on the hunt for the gay men which further emphasizes a present othering of homosexual individuals. In an article titled “There are no others” the author writes that “othering is any action by which an individual or group becomes mentally classified in somebody’s mind as “not one of us” (Othering101, 2011). This idea of othering takes place in many forms not only in terms of sexuality but also in terms of religious practice textually.

The lyrics in the text read “everybody’s disapproval should have worshipped her sooner”. In this line it is clear that because of disapproval she is being othered in terms of her individual views and beliefs. One can even argue that the entire video was created to demonstrate othering but when an image has two different levels of audio and video, the true meaning of the video can be misread. In the end of the video one of the gay men is forced to watch his lover being beat at the base of the fire. This is a great example which challenges the othering of homosexuality in religion as well because through the use of signs it creates a signifier to signify the sin of the gay man.

Anchorage was very clear in this text which is why I believe it to be one of the central concepts of Hozier’s video. The importance of the lyrics within the song were key to understanding and analyzing this films true meaning. At the beginning of the video we can hear Hozier referring to a female but on the screen we are seeing only gay men in a relationship. Why might this be? So after a little digging, I came across an article which spoke of the strict anti-sexual crusade organized by Russian communist party and the Russian Orthodox Church (Kon, 2015).  I wanted to see if there could have been a specific incident which lead the artist to write about sexual orientation and religion. I did end up finding dates close enough to Hozier’s release date where sexual orientation and religion were in the news. I believe that this topic on relationships in religion had a big influence as to its content.

Relay takes place when an image relies on a textual aspect in order to relay any meaning at all (Light, 2016). A good example I can think of which comes to mind is when the lyrics mention anything that is church. Being that there are no images to go along with that text, the viewer must use some skill to decipher both at one time.

Implications

Collectively in its entirety, Hoziers “Take Me To Church” music video has impacted society in a number of ways. As sad as it may be, we often only act when we see graphic details of a wicked issue that deserves some action or if we are directly involved as a stakeholder or victim of the issue. This video puts everything on the table for us to analyze and see an important issue in a light that we rarely get to see. Bringing in the well-known rhetorical concept of Pathos is just what this video needed to ignite implications for knowledge and protest.

As a society we often listen to music without truly listening to the lyrics of the song. We need to learn to think rhetorically in a world where just about everything we need is at our feet. The images in this video can influence many people in terms of how they respect and sympathize with the victims involved as well as shed light on the harsh reality of realizing just how unrealistic religious beliefs can be. The narrative and aesthetics of the video mirror each other creating a feeling which articulates a memorable experience that can in turn provide awareness for the issue at hand.

The ideologies and views within Hozier’s video address dominant views in religion which stigmatize the expression of sexuality. Allowing issues such as these to be brought into music and popular culture can influence positive ideas that can work to overshadow the unrealistic boundaries associated with particular religions. When artists focus on the aesthetics within an image and work towards a particular idea of visual rhetoric positive messages that can implement change for an oppressed group will began to form. According to our class lecture, Light states “Aesthetics are the creative practices that work to produce subjectivities, communities, politics and ways of seeing the world and what is possible within it” (Light, 2016). This proves just how influential the aesthetics of an image can be while further exemplifying the effects Hozier’s video has had on people within our society.
