“Dazed and Confused”

A film analysis by Paul Trujillo.

 

In this analysis, I will critically analyze the film Dazed and Confused which was written and directed by Richard Linklater and released in the same year in which I was born, 1993. From early on, I can remember always taking an interest in this film because of its ability to bring to life everything I imagined the 70’s being. Unlike many films I had ever seen at the time, the director of this film chose to invite the audience into a day in the life of a group of teenaged high school kids and that is exactly the experience I was given when I chose to watch this film.

In my analysis of this film I am choosing to examine the mise-en-scene which comprises all that is in each scene to relay a meaning to me as the audience member. As both a fan and critic of the movie, I will look closely at the placement of the actors in the film, the props chosen and their positions, the costumes of each character and the setting in which these elements exist. As defined by our class text, Ed Sikov states that “Mise-en-scene is the first step in understanding how films produce and reflect meaning”. [1] The author goes on to say, “it is a term taken from the French and it means that which has been put into the scene or put onstage”.[2] Throughout the class text, we see multiple definitions of mise-en-scene because it is literally everything within the filmed image. I will describe this term in its totality with direct examples to give a better understanding of its pertinence within Dazed and Confused. 

            Before I attempt to explain this film and what it’s about, let’s think about the name of the film in and of itself. Dazed and Confused. Say I hadn’t seen or heard of this film and only read the name for the first time written across its cover, which gives hippie vibes of multiple colors and “groovy” writing. I was not born in the 70s, but what’s the first thing that comes to mind when I think of a film baring the title Dazed and Confused? I am taken back to high school experimenting with marijuana for the first time and trying my best to be the “cool kid” who knows where all the parties are. I am stoned, ditching lunch to hit the burrito stand and getting caught only to spend what was left of my week stuck in detention eating lunch with Mr. Taylor. With that said, I knew very well what I was in for when I sat down to watch Dazed and Confused.

In short, this film follows a handful of groups and individuals in high school facing some of what they think are the best and worst times of their lives on a specific day in 1978. From the cool kids to the nerds, we get a picture that parallels their triumphs and trials of high school life. The film opens with a classic rock song and some joint rolling, which couldn’t have hit the 70’s era any better. The main characters in the film are both incoming freshman and graduating juniors and seniors who have reached their last day of school before summer vacation.

As the story unfolds, we see the older characters begin to plan a party to celebrate and the upcoming freshman plot their escape routes to avoid being paddled by the upperclassman. Throughout the film, director Richard Linklater chose to create a story that follows each individual and eventually brings it all together in the end inviting each character to reach some sort of triumph whether it be getting the girl, finding the party or winning the fight of the year while tying each of their actions together. This is not a film with a satisfying ending I can tell you that, in fact it might leave you thinking, “so that’s it”?

Now let’s think about the opening credits of the film and the characters we see in the filmed image. A pan moving into different scenes introduces us to each character whose story we will follow throughout the film. The characters are spending time preparing for their last day of class whether that be smoking a joint, throwing dice, or playing with cards before the last bell rings. Muscle cars of the time and the costumes of each character are direct clues as to what I am in for when the film begins. As the pan begins to slow, a cement wall reads “Lee High School”. On the surface, these factors tell me I am following young adults in the 70s during their time in high school. That cement wall that reads the name of the high school in a single image on the screen tells me what I am in for as the viewer. The images that I see before the cement wall play a part in the mise-en-scene here because they add more context to that single images meaning.

Within every individual and sequence of shots throughout the film, the mise-en-scene relayed a sense of nostalgia to me as the viewer. Nostalgia not only to live in the 70s but to be back in high school with what seemed like a deeper meaning within group interaction. At one point in the film, a group of four friends can be seen sitting around a table in a bedroom with seating. Their shoes are off; they are laughing and enjoying one another’s company; they trust each other. How do I know this from one single look at the shot? The mise-en-scene of this single shot allows me to feel a certain way and take just what the director intended which tells me something beyond what one might only see as people sitting in a room talking.

            All throughout the film the director has a way of pulling us in with the story through the power of the shot. As our text by Sikov states, “a shot is a unit of length or duration – a minimal unit of dramatic material; a scene is a longer unit usually consisting of several shots or more.”[3] “Mise-en-scene describes the content not only of a sequence of shots but of an individual shot.”[4] In this film, the story relies heavily on the sequence of shots to create the full mise-en-scene and relay something beyond the actions of the characters.

            While I was watching this film, I couldn’t help but feel as though the writer wanted to go beyond the usual romantic comedy of the time. As the story plays out, Mitch Kramer and Sabrina, two freshman characters in the film, take a liking to one another at one point but the director chooses not to let their relationship be their story. Although there are parts in this film where relationships build and sexual tensions rise, the director chose to provide us with a small portion of those same images in films of the time which followed the single fling of two people. While doing so, the director also chooses to go against that norm. He continues with a story that falls out of a single relationship between a man and a woman that we are used to seeing in the films of that time in 1993 when this film was released.

            When thinking about the placement of the characters in Dazed and Confused and the setting in which they interacted I think the film does a good job relaying the relationship each character has with one another. Whether the characters are gathered in a circle in the parking lot before the bell rings, seated in the car driving around, or playing pool at a bar, I can build off those images and build a context beyond the script. As I stated before, these inferences come from the abilities of the mise-en-scene in the film.

Looking beyond the character’s ability to relay the story through a script is when one can truly understand the power of mise-en-scene. One of the most used settings within this film consisted of the characters being in a car driving around at night. These scenes showed a wide shot inside the vehicles of each group which brings me to the importance of subject-camera distance. In each scene inside the car the directors chose to use a long-shot that showed every person in the space being the vehicle. Our text describes the importance of subject camera distance as being highly important to relay emotion in certain scenes. The book by Sikov states, “In general, the closer the camera is to the subject, the more emotional weigh the subject gains.”[5]

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

            As a student of this class and as an amateur film critic, I have tried to find deeper meaning within films such as these that do not have meaning spelled out for you to see. I found this film a bit harder to analyze because there isn’t much of an underlying issues the characters are trying to face or overcome. I think it’s harder to find these underlying issues when all that comprises the scene is done in one single day throughout the story. It was not until I took a step back and thought about the age of the characters and the times in which this story takes place that I truly began to see that there was something that the director wanted to relay in this film.

            This films lack in structure truly plays a vital role in exactly what the director had intended the film be. It was a time life seemed so simple yet so unraveled that you thought it that you may “look back and think they were the worst times of your life”, as quoted by the main young character in the film, Mitch Kramer. There are points in this film where we can piece together the innocence of Mitch’s character solely on the Mise-en-scene which encompasses the filmed image. At one point, he comes home, we see his mother waiting by the door seemingly worried like she had been waiting up for him. In this scene, we can infer that it is not like Mitch to come home this late. We then see mitch sort of stumble through the door we know that he has had a few drinks and his mother has noticed. She is standing in the scene with her back towards camera 1, arms crossed at her chest while Mitch gives her the look of “I’m innocent”. Being that this scene comes into nearing its end, the director brings the story to a full circle. I think that he wanted to relay to us the innocence of the time. As viewers, we were able to watch the innocence of Mitch unfold as he attends his first party and takes his first drink of alcohol. He has his first high school romance and a ticket to the “cool kids” group. Through cinematic choices, the director invited the use of props, costumes, and character to relay to us that shift in a young man’s life that happens so quickly when a boy reaches high school. The closing scene of the film where Mitch is seen laying in his bed with a room full of props that scream innocence of being a child, we truly understand what has happened. Mitch has grown up and is ready to leave his innocence behind as he enters the new chapter of his life, that being high school.

            There are also many other parts in the film that work to follow that 70s feel we all see in Dazed and Confused from the opening credits to the closing of the film. Let’s take the beginning of the film for instance, we see classic cars of the time, long hair on guys, tight jeans, and big hair on girls with bright makeup and cut off shorts. These elements come together to create the groovy feel we all know and love in this film. Along with the score of the film, you can guess that the director wanted to depict that time so perfectly that we wanted to take part in all the films actions or parties ourselves. This film has a mise-en-scene so good, I feel like I am right there in the center of it all.

            Taking a deeper look at the props and setting used in the film allows us to truly appreciate the time as well. At one point I remember when the friends sit around in a circle in a room smoking a joint, you see funky furniture that one does not usually see today. A white plastic bubble chair hangs from the ceiling while cubed box shelves hang on the walls in the room. These props played a significant role as to the time and the feeling I got from this film. A feeling of relaxation and good vibes. I mean look “dude”, aren’t I already talking like a person from the 70s just sitting here typing this paper? The director has a way of bringing viewers in a way that is inviting and comforting even when there isn’t a great story line to follow within the single day that is portrayed in the movie.

I think that the setting of the characters played a big role in the mise-en-scene as well because it allowed us to hear, see and even at times feel the atmosphere of the time. The bowling alley scene in the film captures the time so perfectly that I wanted to dance as I heard the music playing in the background. Here, we see a character played by Matthew McConougheys leaned us against the wall as the camera pans slowly to the left while following the beer in his hands being passed to his other buddies, all the while, the classic hit song “slow ride” can be heard. Shots like these are exactly what puts us as the viewers there in the scene. When I can follow the beer being passed around as I watch, I somehow feel and want a beer as well. In the background of this same scene we see the character up against a bowling alley mural draped in color. In the 70’s, the hippie was in full fledge and so were their groovy colors. This film did not lack any of the hippie colors of its time in any cinematic image.

            The actors chosen in this film could not have played their roles perfectly. Several well-known stars were seen including Ben Afflec, Jason London, Adam Goldberg and Joey Lauren Adams. From the gestures and their uncaring attitudes, each character played a role in the films intentions. They were carefree and happy which I think is exactly what made this film so enjoyable. It’s a film where you don’t have to critically think about what is going on to understand because there isn’t much to understand other than a day in the life of a group of chill  high school students. The director wanted to take us back in time through a sense of nostalgia or a desire to wish we were there even if we could not experience the time.

            Putting all the elements of the placement of the actors in the film, the props chosen and their positions, the costumes of each character and the setting in which these elements exist one can come to a base conclusion as to why this film was created. I believe that the director wanted us to feel both a sense of belonging as well as the empathy of maturing into the adults we are today. Each scene allows us to think back to the good times in our lives which has made us who we are today. Each point in the story makes us feel like we can relate because we have been through those same trials and triumphs. We remember our first beer and our first kiss because of films like these. We don’t think about times in our lives that happened in the past otherwise so I feel that Richard Linklater wanted us to be able to relate back to those great times in our lives and feel happy because it’s a good feeling to be able to appreciate your past when you are given reason to think about all the great times. What better time than high school are we carefree, happy and in great times? I cannot think of a better way to tell a story whose mise-en-scene allows me to relate so much with the characters that I feel like I am in the film myself living each trial and triumph.
